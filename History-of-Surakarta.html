History of Surakarta

The city's existence began when Sinuhun Paku Buwana II, king of the Islamic Mataram Sultanate, moved the royal seat from Kartasura to Sala Village, a village not far from the banks of the Bengawan Solo River, after the Kartasura palace was destroyed by a rebel attack.

Sunan Pakubuwana II purchased land from the village head of Sala Village, Kyai Sala, for 10,000 ringgit (Dutch guilders) to build a new Mataram palace. Officially, the new Islamic Mataram palace was named the Surakarta Hadiningrat Palace and occupancy began on February 20, 1745.

The Giyanti Treaty, signed by Sinuhun Paku Buwana III, the Dutch, and Prince Mangkubumi on February 13, 1755, divided the Mataram region into two: the Kasunanan Surakarta and the Sultanate of Yogyakarta. The territory of the Kasunanan Surakarta further shrank due to the Treaty of Salatiga, held on March 17, 1757, which resulted in Raden Mas Said being recognized as an independent prince with a duchy, called the Mangkunegaran Surakarta Duchy (Pura Mangkunegaran Surakarta). As ruler of Mangkunegaran, Raden Mas Said held the title of Adipati Mangkunegara I.
